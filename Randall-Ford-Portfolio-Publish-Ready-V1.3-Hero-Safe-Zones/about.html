Listen thoughtfully, make sense of complexity, and take action to create meaningful value.

Product management, branding, and marketing provide countless opportunities to overcome challenges through creative problem-solving. Those opportunities have helped me develop a thoughtful, broadly informed approach that can often lead to unexpected creative solutions.

The throughline

Audience insight, strategic clarity, systems thinking, cross-functional leadership, and brand stewardship.

Throughout my career, I've seized opportunities that have shaped both my perspective and my approach. I've worked across entertainment, gaming, digital collectibles, mobile products, licensed brands, and resident technology, but the throughline has remained consistent: listen thoughtfully, make sense of complexity, and take action to create meaningful value.

01

Understanding people comes first.

My professional path has not been entirely linear. I began in marketing, where I learned how brands communicate, how communities form around products, and how strongly people can connect with the stories, characters, and experiences they love. Working in entertainment taught me that fans are not passive consumers. They notice the details. They understand the source material. They care whether a product feels authentic, and they are not shy about offering their perspective—which can be incredibly valuable when developing products.

That customer perspective became the foundation of how I approach product work. Whether I am examining purchasing behavior in a digital collectibles app, helping residents navigate a major technology transition, or developing products around a licensed property, I begin by trying to understand the experience from the user's side. What are they trying to accomplish? Where are they getting stuck? What expectations are they bringing with them? Which signals are hidden beneath the surface-level metrics?

02

I am drawn to unclear situations.

The other major theme in my career has been stepping into situations where the path forward is unclear. I have inherited products with declining performance and no agreed-upon explanation. I have helped build new digital-product channels without established processes or ownership. I have worked through acquisitions, platform migrations, evolving requirements, and transitions in which critical knowledge or responsibility could easily have been lost.

Those are often the situations where I've done the work I'm most proud of.

I like taking a complicated problem and giving it shape. That may mean building a forecast, mapping dependencies, creating a product roadmap, establishing a meeting cadence, clarifying ownership, or translating customer behavior into a strategy that people across the organization can understand. The system itself is not the goal. The goal is to create enough clarity to empower teams to make better decisions and execute with confidence.

03

Systems only matter if people can use them.

That work is deeply human. A good analysis does not create change on its own. People need to believe in the direction, understand their role in it, and trust the person asking them to change how they work. Some of the most meaningful moments in my career have involved bringing calm to high-pressure conversations, earning support from skeptical stakeholders, building productive relationships across departments, and helping teams with different priorities work toward the same outcome.

04

Stewardship matters.

My experience with major entertainment brands has also taught me the importance of stewardship. Working with properties such as Marvel, Rick and Morty, Adventure Time, Naruto, and Attack on Titan required more than following a checklist. It required understanding why each brand mattered to its audience and translating detailed creative, legal, and commercial requirements into products that felt true to the property.

I carried that same mindset into the continued development of Cryptozoic's original Cryptkins IP. When stewardship of the brand became unclear, I saw an opportunity to help give it a stronger foundation—creating guardrails, developing new content and product concepts, and advocating for its long-term potential. That experience reinforced something I have come to believe strongly: good product leadership is not only about launching the next thing. It is also about recognizing what is worth protecting and creating the conditions for it to grow.

05

Working between disciplines.

Across these experiences, I have become comfortable working between disciplines. I can speak with creative teams about character and visual identity, with engineers about requirements and constraints, with finance about forecasts and commercial performance, with licensors about compliance and brand integrity, and with executives about risk, strategy, and outcomes.

I bring analytical thinking, but I know that data needs a meaningful narrative. I bring process, but I do not believe process should become bureaucracy. I care about commercial results, but I also believe the most sustainable results come from understanding the people a product is meant to serve.

At the core of my work is that same approach: listen thoughtfully, make sense of complexity, and take action to create meaningful value.
